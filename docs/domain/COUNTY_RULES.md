# Hillsborough Judicial Sales & Foreclosure Rules

## Governing Law
Judicial sales in Hillsborough County are fundamentally regulated by the Florida Statutes, specifically **FS 45.031 (Judicial Sales Procedure)** and **FS 45.032 (Disbursement of Surplus Funds)**, combined with local Thirteenth Judicial Circuit Administrative Orders.

## The Auction Process

### 1. Notice of Sale
- The court’s final judgment directs the clerk to sell the property at public auction.
- The sale date must be scheduled no less than 20 days and no more than 35 days after the judgment date (though plaintiff delays can extend this).
- The Notice of Sale must be published once a week for two consecutive weeks in a local newspaper of general circulation, with the second publication at least 5 days before the sale.

### 2. Bidding & Deposits (FS 45.031)
- Hillsborough County conducts these sales entirely online via the third-party vendor RealAuction (`www.hillsborough.realforeclose.com`).
- **Deposit:** To bid on a property, a bidder must register on the site and place a deposit prior to the sale.
- **Winning Bid:** The winning high bidder must provide a deposit equal to **5% of their final bid**. This amount is applied to the final sale price.
- **Title Condition:** Properties are sold strictly "AS IS". A certificate of title issued after a judicial sale is *not* warranted to be free of all potential claims or surviving liens (e.g., code enforcement, superior mortgages, taxes). Buyer beware applies absolutely.

### 3. Final Payment & Default
- If the winning bidder fails to pay the remaining balance within the specified window, the clerk will cancel the transaction and readvertise the sale. 
- The initial 5% deposit is forfeit. It is used to cover the costs of the failed sale, with any remaining amount applied toward the plaintiff's original judgment debt.

### 4. Objections to Sale
- Any party to the case may formally object to the amount of the bid or the sale procedure within **10 days** after the clerk files the Certificate of Sale.
- If no objections are filed within this 10 day window, the clerk issues the Certificate of Title, transferring ownership.

### 5. Sale Cancellations
A judicial sale in Hillsborough can be canceled prior to the auction for several reasons, including:
- A Notice of Cancellation filed by the Plaintiff no later than 8:00 AM on the day of the sale.
- Failure of the Plaintiff to pay the $70 judicial sale fee by 8:00 AM on the day of the sale.
- A timely bankruptcy petition filed by the defendant (Suggestion of Bankruptcy).
- An explicit written court order.
- Significant errors in the published Notice of Sale.

## Court Registry & Fees

When money moves through the Hillsborough Clerk’s Court Registry (including foreclosure escrow and surplus deposits), the clerk assesses statutory administrative fees:

- **Registry Fee:** The Clerk takes a fee of **3% for the first $500**, and **1.5% for each subsequent $100** deposited into the registry.
- **Sale Service Charge:** The Plaintiff must pay a $70 service charge for filing an electronic sale date.

## Surplus Funds (FS 45.032)

If a property sells at auction for more than the outstanding foreclosing judgment debt (plus associated costs), the excess money is legally classified as "Surplus Funds".

### 1. Priority of Claims
1. **Subordinate Lienholders:** Holders of junior liens (e.g., second mortgages, HOA liens, judgment liens) that were named in the foreclosure pleadings have the absolute first right to claim the surplus to satisfy their debts.
2. **Owner of Record:** Once all subordinate lienholders are paid, the remaining surplus belongs to the "owner of record" as of the date the original Lis Pendens was filed. 

### 2. Claim Process & Timelines
- Claimants typically must file a claim for surplus funds within **120 days** of the Clerk issuing the notice of available surplus.
- Property owners may assign their right to surplus funds to third-party recovery agencies, but this assignment must be approved by the court.
- In the event of competing claims, the court holds a hearing to determine legal entitlement.

### 3. Unclaimed Funds
- If no claim is made within the 120-day window, the Clerk transfers the surplus to the Florida Department of Financial Services (Division of Unclaimed Property).
- Any funds remaining with the clerk after 1 year are presumed permanently unclaimed and remitted to the state. The former owner can still file a claim through the state thereafter, but the process is significantly slower.
